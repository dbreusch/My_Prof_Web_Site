Teaching

This page summarizes my thoughts on teaching, including my "vision" for an approach to teaching, my interest areas, my experience in the classroom, and my work with students (graduate and undergraduate, committees and advising).

Teaching vision

I believe that as a teacher in climate science and global change that my students should be challenged by these four main goals: (1) understand the main elements of the climate system and their interactions, including a thorough historical perspective; (2) recognize the common misconceptions about the evidence for climate change and global warming (i.e., the skeptics arguments); (3) understand the uncertainties of observations and modeling, both inherent and avoidable; and (4) develop a sincere interest in science, what it means to “do science” (i.e., the process), and an appreciation of the societal benefits of science.

The depth and breadth to which these goals are pursued will of course vary by the nature of the student. For non-science majors, perhaps taking their only course in this subject area (or in science at all), it is important to reach a basic competency that will help them in future activities where science is relevant (e.g., should I buy that beachfront property? does this politician value science? how can events at the poles possibly affect the weather where I live?). In short, I want them to leave my class with a set of basic skills that will help them engage with these important ideas, have some confidence in making decisions where climate change is a factor, and recognize invalid scientific arguments in the climate change debate.

Students in courses for majors will be expected to dig deeper into these topics and the bar for achieving each goal will naturally be higher. Topics will also be expanded in breadth, e.g., covering more of the skeptics arguments or additional details of the climate system. Upper-level courses will also have more collaborative learning where students work with each other to develop deeper understanding, e.g., by having to explain things to their peers (how often have we heard that we don’t really understand something until we try to explain it to someone else). In a writing-intensive course, this would include anonymous peer review of student writing, an activity that helps students prepare for the experience of publishing their own research in journals. There is also scope for student-led original research, either as a group activity or as part of a student’s personal curriculum (for graduate students or advanced undergraduates).

Climate science, particularly in the polar regions, provides many opportunities for student engagement. Whether it be something easily related to, like the threat to polar bear or penguin habitat, or more involved, like feedbacks between sea ice and winter storminess in the U.S., there are many ways to connect students with the science. News headlines from recent record Arctic sea-ice minima (or less recently, the impact of Icelandic volcanic eruptions on air travel) provide ready topics for class discussion and study. Climate events and processes such as ENSO, drought, hurricanes, and winter storms are also out there waiting to be brought into students’ lives – and thereby help them connect with both climate science in particular and the more general “idea” of science and its benefits to society.

My teaching vision also includes the teacher learning to be a better teacher. Any given approach to teaching a topic is unlikely to be born perfect. As our understanding of a topic changes, so may our approach to sharing it with students. Just as our scientific methodologies evolve and improve as our skills mature, so should our teaching methods. After all, teaching is both an interactive and two-way process that should also include learning from our students (the most polished lecture does little good if it doesn’t connect with the students).

Teaching program/interests

My teaching interests broadly follow my research interests and encompass the following topic areas: (1) components and importance of the global climate system; (2) climate change, past, present and future, including paleoclimatology; (3) history of climate change science; (4) practical computing skills for student research and data analysis.

My experience to date has been in “global climates”, i.e., what is the climate system and what mechanisms and processes are responsible for its fluctuations on different spatial and temporal scales. Lectures cover the atmosphere (structure, composition, temperature, moisture, circulation), the cryosphere (glaciers, sea ice, ice-albedo feedback), hydrosphere (ocean circulation, hydrologic cycle), why the Earth has liquid water (energy balance), teleconnections (El Niño-Southern Oscillation, North Atlantic Oscillation), short- and long-term records of climate (observations, proxies, landscape), and some climate modeling. My background in ice-core science, polar meteorology/climatology, and regional climate modeling also brings additional perspectives.

At Penn State I taught versions of this material in two different class settings in the Geography department. The smaller (15-20 student) class covers material more in-depth and is structured for meeting Penn State requirements for a writing-intensive course, i.e., regular writing assignments of various types, an essay midterm and a final paper. Effective written communication skills are a key part of undergraduate (and graduate!) education adding important value to such a course. As at Penn State, this course would be well-suited for department majors.

The larger version of this course (up to 80 students) is general education oriented, focuses more on the atmospheric sciences and has a weekly lab (I had two TAs for the labs and occasional lectures). It would make a nice course for non-majors and provide a “science lab” experience (oriented towards the data analysis side of climate science).

I am also interested in taking the material from these courses and reframing it into a course that might be called “Quaternary Paleoclimatology” or “Introduction to Paleoclimatology”. That is, a course focused more on paleoclimatology (changes over longer times, sources of data and interpretation, long-term proxies, how the past may help us predict the future, etc.), data analysis techniques, and Earth history from a geological perspective, at global and/or regional scope. This course could also be organized as a seminar-style class with more emphasis on foundational and recent literature, e.g., as a “Special Topics” course in Climatology.

Longer term, I am interested in developing a seminar-type course based on the 2011 book “The Warming Papers: The Scientific Foundation for the Climate Change Forecast”, by David Archer & Raymond Pierrehumbert. This compendium of foundational papers related to greenhouse gases, climate modeling, global warming and climate change offers an opportunity to give students a multicentury perspective on these topics, from Fourier (1824), Tyndall (1861), and climate icon Svante Arrhenius (1896), to key developments of recent decades. Although this material is accessible to undergraduates with the right background, it might be best suited as a graduate-level offering.

Stepping away a bit from climate science, another potential course of interest involves less science and more practical computing skills for students, i.e., what do students in the geosciences, for example, need to know about computing to be effective researchers. A course like this, known as “Anything but Excel” at Penn State, covers topics such as how data are stored, making life easier with Unix scripting, why basic editors are better than using Word for text files, and how to do data plotting and basic exploration with Matlab, GMT (Generic Mapping Tools) and other applications more appropriate than Excel. Although this is a “course of the future” for me, my computer science and research experience should provide sufficient background.

Lastly, my extensive research experience with artificial neural networks (e.g., in downscaling reanalysis data to AWS sites) and self-organizing maps (SOMs) certainly has the makings of a new course on applying such tools in meteorology and climate science. Such a course (or seminar) would primarily involve material focused on methodology (i.e., data preparation, execution of the tool, postprocessing and interpretation) accompanied by a range of applications from the literature. Students would also have hands-on experience with these tools.

In summary, my teaching experience broadly covers “the climate system”, a field of wide scientific interest and important societal implications. My existing courses suit department undergraduate and graduate students as well as broader community interests. Potential future courses further develop these ideas.
